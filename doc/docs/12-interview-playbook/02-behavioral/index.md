# 02 · Behavioral

> Build a story bank of 6-8 STAR stories. They get re-used across rounds and companies.

The behavioral round is the most predictable part of any interview loop. The questions look infinite — "tell me about a time you led without authority / disagreed with your manager / failed / shipped under pressure / handled an ambiguous problem" — but they map onto a small set of *story categories*. Pre-write 6-8 stories that cover those categories once, and you're prepared for every behavioral round at every company forever.

## The story bank approach

A story bank is a written collection of 6-8 of your own past projects, indexed by the *behavioral signals they demonstrate*. The same project can usually serve 2-3 different categories — a redesign that you led, that almost slipped, and that ultimately doubled a metric is simultaneously a leadership, failure, and impact story depending on which angle you tell.

Aim for coverage across these signals: **leadership, failure, conflict, ambiguity, deadline pressure, technical depth, cross-team influence, mentorship, customer/user focus, ethical or values-driven choice.** Most candidates ship 6-8 stories that, combined, cover all of them.

Write each story once in long-form (300-500 words), then practice a 90-second version of each. Spend twice as long on the *reflection* — the "what I'd do differently" or "what I learned" tail — as on the action. That's the part interviewers grade hardest at senior levels.

## How behavioral rounds actually work

The interviewer typically has 3-5 questions to get through in 45-60 minutes. They're scoring on a rubric — most companies use some version of: *did the candidate demonstrate ownership, ambition, judgment, communication, and self-awareness.* They are *not* trying to trick you; they want clean, well-structured stories they can score quickly. Helping them score you well is in your interest.

The single highest-leverage move in any behavioral round is to **lead with the result**: "Sure, I can tell you about a time I shipped under pressure — the punchline is we delivered a 6-week project in 4 weeks by cutting scope twice and the launch hit our quarterly revenue goal. Let me walk you through it." Now the interviewer knows where you're going and can listen for the *how*, instead of waiting through 90 seconds of setup before they know if the story even hits the question.
