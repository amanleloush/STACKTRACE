# 02 — Leadership Stories

> Behavioral • Position 2/5

## The question family

Leadership questions are the ones interviewers use to test whether you operate above your title. At staff and principal levels they're often the deciding factor. They sound like:

- "Tell me about a time you led without authority."
- "Describe a project where you influenced a decision outside your team."
- "Walk me through a time you mentored someone."
- "Tell me about the most complex project you've led."
- "Describe a time you drove alignment across a disagreement."
- "Have you ever taken on something that wasn't formally your responsibility?"

The interviewer wants to know whether you wait for permission or whether you see a gap and step into it. The best leadership stories are almost never about being a manager — they're about IC moments where you decided the team needed something and you went and made it happen.

## The framework

Use **STAR-Plus with explicit influence framing.** The pieces specific to leadership stories:

1. **Show the gap.** Strong leadership stories start with a problem nobody had been formally assigned. *"Nobody owned X yet"* or *"the project was stalling and there was no single decision-maker."* Without the gap, the story is just "I did my job."
2. **Show the choice to step in.** What made you the right person? Why did you decide to act rather than escalate or wait?
3. **Show the influence mechanism.** *How* did you actually move other people? The mechanisms matter: writing a doc, running a meeting, pairing with someone, escalating, pulling in a sponsor, building a prototype.
4. **Show that other people moved.** The result has to include the *response* of the people you were leading — not just the project outcome. "And then three other teams adopted the pattern" is the kind of result that grades well.
5. **Reflection on the leadership move itself.** Not "what we learned about the project" — what you learned about *leading*.

The dominant failure mode is telling a "leadership" story that's really just a personal-accomplishment story. The interviewer is grading you on *getting other people to do things*, not on doing impressive things yourself.

## Story template

> *(Gap)* "About a year ago, our org was running three separate **observability platforms** — Datadog for some services, an in-house Prometheus setup for others, and a third-party tracing tool nobody fully owned. **It wasn't anyone's job to consolidate them**, but I'd been on call enough to know we were spending a meaningful amount of incident time just figuring out which dashboard to look at.
>
> *(Choice)* The instinct was to escalate to leadership and ask for a project to be created. **I decided against that** because I'd seen similar consolidation requests stall in our planning process for months. Instead, I started small — **I picked the two services I was most familiar with and wrote a one-page comparison of what an integrated setup would look like**, including a rough estimate of incident-time savings.
>
> *(Influence)* I shared the doc with **three other engineers I knew had felt the same pain**, took their feedback, and turned it into a stronger proposal with a phased rollout. Then I brought it to **our principal engineer** with the framing 'here's a proposal you can either back or shoot down — I'd rather we decide either way than let it drift.' She backed it, helped me get it on the platform team's quarterly roadmap, and **I stayed on as the IC partner driving the design decisions** even though the implementation team owned the build.
>
> *(Result)* The consolidated platform shipped over the following two quarters. **Mean time to detect dropped by about 40% in the quarter after rollout**, and — the part I'm proudest of — **two of the three engineers I'd pulled in early are now leading their own cross-team initiatives** using a similar pattern.
>
> *(Reflection)* What I learned about leading from an IC seat is that **the bottleneck is almost never the work — it's the alignment.** A one-page doc plus three pre-aligned peers is enough to unstick most things that look like they need org-chart authority. I've used that pattern four or five times since."

Roughly 350 words. Notice what makes it score well as a leadership story: a clearly-named gap nobody owned, an explicit deliberation about *how* to step in, a sequence of influence moves (writing the doc, pre-aligning peers, framing for the sponsor), a result that includes both a metric and a *people* outcome, and a reflection that generalizes about leadership rather than the project.

## 3 worked stories

### Senior — leading a cross-team initiative

"Our org needed to consolidate three observability platforms. Nobody owned it. I wrote a one-page comparison, pre-aligned three peer engineers who'd felt the same pain, brought it to our principal engineer with a 'back it or kill it' framing. She backed it; the consolidation shipped over two quarters. **40% drop in MTTD, and two of the engineers I pulled in early now lead their own cross-team initiatives.** The lesson: from an IC seat, the bottleneck is almost never the work, it's the alignment — and a doc plus three pre-aligned peers is usually enough to unstick it."

### Mid-level — leading without authority on your team

"On my team, **we had a code review backlog problem** — average time-to-first-review had drifted to about 36 hours. Nobody was assigned to fix it. I proposed a simple change in our weekly team meeting — **a round-robin assignment instead of self-selection** — and offered to track the metric for a month. The first version of the proposal failed; two senior engineers pushed back because they wanted to review their own areas of expertise. I went back, added a **'domain-routing override'** to address their concern, and re-proposed. Time-to-first-review dropped from 36 hours to about 8 hours over six weeks. **What I learned was that getting buy-in from the senior people on the team mattered more than getting the design right on the first try** — the override existed primarily for them, and that's what unlocked the change."

### Junior — leading a small initiative or mentoring

"As a relatively new engineer on the team, I noticed that **our onboarding docs were significantly out of date** — when I joined, I'd had to debug three setup failures that weren't covered. **Nobody had ownership of the docs**, so a few months after I'd ramped up, I asked my manager if I could spend a week refreshing them. He said yes. I rewrote the setup guide, added a 'common failure modes' section based on the issues I'd hit, and **ran a half-hour walkthrough with the two engineers who joined after me**. Their onboarding to first-PR-merged dropped from about three weeks to under ten days. **What I learned was that being new on a team is itself a leadership advantage for some kinds of work — I had the most recent memory of what was missing.** I now write notes on my own onboarding gaps at every new role so I can make the same kind of contribution early."

## Anti-patterns

- **"Leadership" stories that are really solo-accomplishment stories.** No other people involved = not a leadership story.
- **Title-leadership instead of influence-leadership.** "I was promoted to tech lead" isn't the story. *What you did once you were leading* is the story.
- **Taking sole credit.** Leadership stories where the candidate did literally everything read as either inaccurate or as a sign they can't delegate.
- **No mention of dissent or pushback.** Real leadership work involves convincing people who initially disagreed. Stories without any friction read as fictional.
- **No follow-up on the people side.** "We shipped the project" is half a result. *"And the engineers I mentored have since led their own work"* is the full result.
- **Going over 3 minutes.** Even your most impressive leadership story should fit inside 120-150 seconds.

## Prep checklist

For each leadership story:

- *Who specifically did I lead? Name them mentally.* (Specifics keep you honest.)
- *What was the explicit gap or vacuum I stepped into?*
- *What was my influence mechanism — doc, meeting, prototype, sponsor, escalation?*
- *What's the people-side result, not just the project-side?*
- *What did I learn about leading that I've reused since?*
