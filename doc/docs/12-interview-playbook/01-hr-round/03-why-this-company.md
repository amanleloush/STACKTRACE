# 03 — Why This Company

> HR round • Position 3/6

## The question (and its variants)

This is the homework question. It separates candidates who applied to 40 places this week from candidates who actually want to be here.

- "Why are you interested in *us*?"
- "What attracted you to this role?"
- "Why did you apply?"
- "What do you know about what we do?"
- "Out of all the companies you could be talking to, why this one?"

The harder variant is "Out of all the companies you could be talking to" — it forces a *comparative* answer rather than a generic one.

## What they're actually evaluating

- **Did you do at least 20 minutes of homework?** The threshold is low but most candidates fail it.
- **Are your reasons specific?** "I love the mission" without a concrete reason behind it is filler.
- **Do your stated motivations match what this company actually offers?** Wanting "high autonomy" at a tightly-managed FAANG is a flag.
- **Is the motivation sustainable?** "I want to learn X" works only if X is something the role actually involves.

## The framework

Use a **2-3 pillar structure**, each pillar tied to a motivation of yours that this company specifically satisfies. The pillars can come from this menu — pick the ones that genuinely apply:

- **Product / mission** — you're a user, or you care about the problem space.
- **Technical scale or interestingness** — the engineering challenges line up with what you want to be doing.
- **Team / people** — you've talked to engineers there, or you respect specific senior people in the org.
- **Stage / growth trajectory** — early enough to have impact, mature enough to be stable, growing fast enough to create opportunity.
- **Culture signal** — eng-led, design-led, write-things-down culture, low-meeting culture, whatever maps to how you actually work.
- **Personal alignment** — geography, remote policy, family stage. Optional and last if used at all.

Two strong pillars beat four weak ones. Each pillar should be one sentence of *what attracts you* plus one sentence of *why that maps to you personally*.

## Strong answer template

> "Three things pulled me into the loop. First, **the product**. I've been a user of yours for about two years and the thing I've always respected is that **the team ships fewer features than your competitors but each one is well-considered** — that's a culture signal that tells me there's room to do my best work and not just churn out shovelware. Second, **the technical scope**. The job description mentioned you're rebuilding the **identity and entitlements platform from scratch**, and I read **your engineering blog post from last quarter about your migration off the monolithic auth service**. Greenfield work on a platform that touches every other team is exactly the leverage I'm optimizing for in my next role. Third, **the people**. I had a coffee with **one of your senior engineers** at a meetup six months ago, and the way she described how design decisions get made — written proposals, async review, weekly architecture forum — lined up with the working style I've been trying to find. The combination of those three is rare enough that I made this loop my top priority this quarter."

Roughly 190 words. The **bolded** parts are what makes this answer credible: a *specific* product observation (not "I love your product"), a *specific* technical reference (the blog post, the named system), and a *specific* person interaction. Generic versions of all three exist; this version proves you actually did the work.

## Anti-patterns

- **"I've heard great things about your culture."** From whom? About what? Nothing here is verifiable.
- **"I want to work on cutting-edge AI / distributed systems / blockchain."** Generic enough to apply to any company in the space. Reads as filler.
- **"You're a top-tier company and I want to grow my career here."** Reads as flattery, signals you didn't do the homework.
- **Reasons that are really about you, not them.** "I want to live in San Francisco" is true but doesn't answer the question.
- **Bashing your current company as a motivation.** "Why this company" should be a *pull*, not a *push*. The push goes in the "why are you leaving" answer.
- **Naming a product feature that no longer exists.** Embarrassing. Spend 10 minutes on the product the day before the call.

## 30-second elevator version

> "Three reasons. I've been a user for two years and respect the deliberate shipping cadence. The job posting and your recent eng blog post on the auth-platform rebuild line up almost exactly with the kind of greenfield platform work I want to be doing. And the engineer I met at a meetup six months ago described an async, written-design culture that matches how I do my best work. The combination made this my top-priority loop this quarter."

## How to actually do the homework

You need 20-30 minutes per company to make this answer credible. The minimum-viable homework:

1. **Read the careers page** for the company narrative they tell themselves.
2. **Read the most recent two engineering blog posts** — they tell you what the org is currently obsessed with.
3. **Read the most recent funding/earnings announcement** — tells you the strategic phase.
4. **Look up the hiring manager and 2-3 engineers on the team** on LinkedIn — tells you the team's actual seniority and tenure mix.
5. **Use the product for 15 minutes** — even if it's enterprise, find a demo or trial. Real users notice things demos don't.

That's it. Most candidates skip step 5 and it shows immediately.
