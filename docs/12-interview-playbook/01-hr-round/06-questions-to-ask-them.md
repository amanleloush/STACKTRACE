# 06 — Questions to Ask Them

> HR round • Position 6/6

## The question (and its variants)

Every interview ends with some version of:

- "What questions do you have for me?"
- "Anything you'd like to ask before we wrap up?"
- "What can I help clarify?"

This is the most-undervalued segment of the HR call. Most candidates treat it as a closing formality and ask one weak question; strong candidates use the last 5-10 minutes to (a) gather information that decides whether the role is right for them, (b) signal seniority and thoughtfulness, and (c) plant questions that make the recruiter advocate for them internally.

## What they're actually evaluating

- **What do you actually care about?** Your questions reveal your real priorities better than any answer you gave earlier.
- **Are you interview-savvy?** Asking thoughtful, role-specific questions reads as senior.
- **Are you evaluating *them*?** Strong candidates make it clear they have other options and are running a real comparison.
- **Will you be a high-maintenance hire?** Don't ask questions that signal you're already negotiating.

## The framework

Have **3-5 questions ready per round**, categorized so you don't repeat across the loop. For the HR round specifically, the recruiter can only answer questions about the **process, the company, comp/benefits, and team-level information they were briefed on** — don't ask deep technical questions, save those for the hiring manager and engineers.

Tier your questions:

1. **High-value (must ask)** — questions whose answers genuinely change whether you'd take the role.
2. **Medium-value (ask if time)** — questions that give useful color but aren't decisive.
3. **Closer (ask last)** — questions that prompt the recruiter to advocate for you ("what would make a candidate successful in this loop").

## Strong answer template

This isn't an answer — it's a *menu*. Pick 3-5 from the list below per round, leaning on category 1 in the HR call and on categories 2-5 in technical rounds.

### Role (HR round, hiring manager)

1. **"What does success look like in this role at 30, 60, and 90 days?"** — surfaces whether the team has a real ramp plan or expects you to figure it out.
2. **"What's the most important thing you're hoping the person in this seat does in the first year?"** — surfaces the *actual* priority vs the job-description priority.
3. **"How is this role different from the previous person's? Is this a backfill or a new seat?"** — tells you if you're replacing a star, replacing someone fired, or filling a new gap.

### Team (HR round, hiring manager, peers)

4. **"How is the team structured today? How has it changed in the last year?"** — tells you about stability and growth trajectory.
5. **"What's the team's biggest technical challenge right now?"** — gives you a preview of what you'd actually work on.
6. **"What's the average tenure on the team?"** — short tenure across the team is a warning sign worth investigating.

### Manager (hiring manager round, sometimes HR)

7. **"How do you give feedback? What's your management style?"** — opens up the real conversation about how you'd actually work together.
8. **"What's the last hard piece of feedback you gave to a senior engineer on your team?"** — separates managers who say "I'm hands-off" from managers who actually have a feedback practice.
9. **"What's the most important quality in someone who's been successful on your team in the past?"** — tells you what they hire for vs what the job description says.

### Culture (HR round, peers)

10. **"How are technical decisions made — by individual ICs, by the team, by leads, by an architecture group?"** — tells you whether design is centralized or distributed, which is a *huge* compatibility signal.
11. **"How do you handle disagreement about technical direction?"** — surfaces the actual decision-making culture, not the stated one.
12. **"What's something that surprised you about working here in your first six months?"** — invites honest, off-script answers. The best signal in the whole list.

### Growth (HR round, hiring manager)

13. **"What does the promotion path look like from this level? What's the typical timeline?"** — appropriate to ask in HR; do not ask "when will I be promoted." Frame as understanding the system.
14. **"What learning and development support is there — conferences, internal mobility, mentorship?"** — signals you're thinking long-term.

### Compensation / process (HR round only — and only late)

15. **"What's the comp band for this level, and how is it structured — base, equity, bonus?"** — appropriate to ask in the HR call, ideally near the end. Don't ask in technical rounds.

### Closer (last 2 minutes of any round)

- **"Is there anything in my background that gives you pause? I'd rather address it now than have it be the reason the loop doesn't move forward."** — flips you from passive to confident, gives the interviewer permission to be honest, and is one of the highest-leverage moves in the entire process.
- **"What advice would you give a candidate who wants to do well in the rest of this loop?"** — invites the recruiter to coach you, which subtly aligns them with your candidacy.

## Anti-patterns

- **"I don't have any questions."** Reads as low interest. Always have something.
- **Questions a 30-second Google search would answer.** "What does your company do?" — disqualifying.
- **Comp / benefits / WFH policy in the technical rounds.** Wrong audience. Save for HR or the offer call.
- **Adversarial questions.** "Why is your Glassdoor rating low?" — even when it's fair, this is the wrong room.
- **Questions that signal you've already decided.** "How quickly can you make the offer?" — sounds like you're shopping competing offers, even when you are.
- **Reading from your notes.** Have your questions on paper, but glance, don't read.

## 30-second elevator version

If you have only one question slot:

> "What's the most important thing you're hoping the person in this seat does in the first year — and how is that different from what's in the job description?"

That single question covers role clarity, team priorities, and the gap between stated and actual expectations — three of the most decision-relevant pieces of information you can extract in any interview.

## Calibration: how many questions per round?

- **15-minute recruiter screen**: 2-3 questions. Lead with the highest-value one. Save comp for the last 60 seconds.
- **45-minute HR or hiring manager round**: 4-5 questions. Mix categories.
- **Final-round / loop closer**: 5-8 questions. This is your last chance to gather information before deciding on an offer, and you should be running it almost like *you're* interviewing *them*.

A useful frame: at the final round, you should know the answers to enough of these questions that if an offer arrives the next day, you can decide within 48 hours without scheduling a follow-up call.
