# 04 — Why Are You Leaving

> HR round • Position 4/6

## The question (and its variants)

The flip side of "why this company." Where the previous question is about *pull*, this one is about *push* — and it's a minefield because the natural human urge is to vent.

- "Why are you looking right now?"
- "What's prompting you to leave your current role?"
- "What's not working at your current company?"
- "Why now? Is something happening at work?"
- "Why did you leave your last role?" (for unemployed candidates)

The trap is honesty. Almost every candidate has a real, valid grievance — a bad manager, a stalled promotion, layoffs, a stagnant team. Saying it directly almost always works against you. The recruiter is listening for one thing: *will this person also say this about us in 18 months?*

## What they're actually evaluating

- **Can you reframe a negative into a forward-looking motivation?** This is a maturity test.
- **Are you running *from* something or *to* something?** Strong candidates are clearly *toward* something.
- **Are there red flags?** Bad reasons: short tenure with no clear story, repeated conflict with managers, comp-only motivation.
- **Is your stated reason consistent with your tenure?** Saying "I'm not growing" after 11 months reads differently than after 4 years.

## The framework

Use the **"Forward-looking framing"** — convert any push factor into the corresponding pull factor.

| Push (don't say) | Pull (say) |
| --- | --- |
| "My manager is a micromanager." | "I'm looking for an environment with more autonomy." |
| "My team is stagnant, no one ships." | "I'm looking for a higher-velocity engineering culture." |
| "Comp is below market." | "I want a role that's compensated commensurate with the scope I'm operating at." |
| "I got passed over for promotion." | "I'm looking for clearer growth paths and more leverage on visible work." |
| "We had layoffs." | "The recent restructuring shifted the team's scope and it's no longer the work I want to be doing." |
| "I'm bored." | "I've grown into and out of my current scope — I want the next 10x challenge." |

The pull-version is rarely a *lie* — it's usually a truer version of the underlying motivation. "My manager is a micromanager" is really "I want more autonomy." The latter is what you actually want; the former is just the local symptom.

Structure: 2-3 sentences, no more. This is **not** a place to be detailed. Long answers here read as venting even when they're not.

## Strong answer template

> "Mostly it comes down to **scope and stage**. I've been at **Company X** for about three years, and the team I'm on has stabilized — the systems I built are running well, the headcount has consolidated, and the kinds of problems I want to be working on next aren't really on the roadmap for the next year or two. **I've grown into and out of the current scope**, and rather than wait passively for the next thing to materialize, I'd rather move toward a team where the **next big platform problem is already on the table**. That's most of it. The secondary thing is that I want to be on a team that's actively scaling — my current org has been roughly flat in headcount for 18 months, and I think I do my best work when I'm growing alongside the team."

Roughly 140 words. The **bolded** parts are what makes this credible: *neutral* framing of the current situation (no villains, no complaints), a *specific* push-to-pull translation ("grown into and out of the current scope"), and an honest secondary reason (team scaling) that humanizes it. There's no one to blame in this answer, but it's also not vague — the recruiter walks away with a clear picture of what motivates the move.

## Anti-patterns

- **Criticizing your current manager.** Universal red flag. Recruiters assume you'll say the same things about your next manager.
- **"Comp is the main reason."** Even when true. Compensation is a *constraint*, not a motivation. State it later in the process, not in the HR call.
- **Long, detailed grievance.** The longer you talk, the more it sounds like venting.
- **"Things are fine, I'm just exploring."** Reads as low intent. Recruiters deprioritize candidates who don't seem motivated to actually move.
- **Contradicting your LinkedIn or resume.** "Looking for growth" with three sub-2-year stints reads as a pattern problem.
- **Mentioning that you were performance-managed out / fired.** If you were, address it directly when asked, but never volunteer it as the reason for leaving in an open-ended question.

## 30-second elevator version

> "I've been at my current company for three years and grown into and out of the scope I joined for — the team is stable, the roadmap has consolidated, and the next platform-scale problem I want to work on isn't really on the table here. Rather than wait, I'd rather move toward a team where that work is happening now."

## The special case: you're currently unemployed

If you were laid off, say so plainly and move on: *"I was part of the restructuring at Company X in [month]. About 15% of engineering was affected, including most of my team. I've spent the time since on [study / open source / consulting / a side project] and now I'm looking for the right full-time role."* That's it. Don't apologize, don't explain why you specifically were chosen, don't speculate on why others weren't. Move directly to forward-looking.

If you were fired for cause, that's a longer conversation that's outside the scope of this page — but the same principle applies: own it briefly, reframe to what you've learned, redirect to forward motion. Never lie. Recruiters do reference checks.

## The special case: you're a short-tenure candidate

If your current role is under 18 months, you'll get pressed harder here. Pre-empt the question: *"I joined Company X in [month] for [reason]. About six months in, [specific thing changed] — [restructuring / scope shift / management change] — and the role is no longer what I signed up for. I'd rather make one clean move now than stay in a role that doesn't match what I joined for."* Specificity is your friend; vagueness reads as a pattern.
