# 01 — Tell Me About Yourself

> HR round • Position 1/6

## The question (and its variants)

The recruiter opens the call with some version of:

- "So, tell me a little bit about yourself."
- "Walk me through your background."
- "Give me a quick intro — what have you been working on?"
- "Take me through your resume."

It's the first question you'll hear in 95% of HR calls and often the first question in technical screens too. It feels casual. It is not. This is the moment the recruiter calibrates which band to slot you into, which team to route you to, and how much energy to invest in your loop. A weak answer here can mean you get downleveled before you've written a line of code.

## What they're actually evaluating

- **Can you self-edit?** A senior engineer summarizes 8 years in 75 seconds. A junior one takes 4 minutes.
- **Do you understand which parts of your story are relevant to *this* role?** Tailoring is a seniority signal.
- **Is there a coherent arc?** Random job-hopping with no narrative reads as "drifting." A clear thread reads as intentional.
- **Are you comfortable talking about yourself without being arrogant?** Soft skill check disguised as a warmup.

## The framework

Use **Present → Past → Future**. Three beats, roughly 25 seconds each, totaling 60-90 seconds.

1. **Present (where you are now)** — current role, company, scope of work, biggest thing you're driving. Lead with the *role* and *impact*, not the company name.
2. **Past (how you got here)** — one or two career pivots that explain why your current scope makes sense. Skip the chronological dump; pick the 2-3 inflection points.
3. **Future (why you're here on this call)** — what you're looking for next and why this role/company maps to it. This is the bridge into "why are you interviewing with us."

You can flip Past and Present if your past is more impressive than your present, but keep Future last — it's the natural handoff to the recruiter's next question.

## Strong answer template

> "I'm currently a senior backend engineer at **Company X**, where I lead the **payments reliability workstream** — a team of four engineers responsible for keeping checkout flows above 99.95% uptime and reducing P99 latency on the critical path. **In the last year I cut payment failures by 40%** by rebuilding our retry layer with idempotency keys and moving our hot path to an event-driven model on Kafka.
>
> Before this I spent three years at **Company Y** working on a low-latency ad-serving system — that's where I learned distributed systems the hard way, on a service doing **150K QPS** with tight latency budgets. I started my career as a generalist at a small startup, which is where I picked up the bias toward **shipping fast and instrumenting everything**, which still shapes how I work.
>
> What's pulling me to look right now is **scope** — I want to work on systems that are an order of magnitude larger than what I see today, with a stronger platform org around them. When I read your team's job description and saw you're rebuilding the **identity/auth platform from scratch with a Kafka-based eventing layer**, that lined up almost exactly with where I want to spend the next few years."

Roughly 175 words. Notice the structure: 25 seconds present (with one quantified result), 25 seconds past (with one quantified result), 20 seconds future (with one *specific* hook into the role you're interviewing for). The bold parts are what a strong candidate hits — quantified scope, named technologies, a clear forward motivation.

## Anti-patterns

- **Reading your resume aloud chronologically.** The recruiter has the resume. Tell them what isn't on it.
- **No quantified scope anywhere.** "I work on backend systems" tells me nothing; "I work on backend systems handling 50K QPS" tells me your level.
- **Ending on "...and that's why I'm here."** Always end on a *forward* statement that hands the conversation back to them.
- **Listing technologies as a CV dump.** "I've worked with Java, Spring, Kafka, Redis, Cassandra, AWS, GCP, Kubernetes, Terraform..." sounds like you're afraid of being filtered on keywords. Pick the 2-3 that matter for this role and weave them into the story.
- **Going over 2 minutes.** If the recruiter has to interrupt you, you've already lost a small amount of credibility.

## 30-second elevator version

> "Senior backend engineer at **Company X**, currently leading payments reliability — cut failure rates 40% in the last year by rebuilding our retry and event pipeline. Before that, three years on a high-QPS ad-serving system at **Company Y**. Looking to move because I want larger scope, and your team's identity platform rebuild is exactly the kind of greenfield distributed-systems work I want to be doing for the next few years."

Use this when the recruiter says "we have 25 minutes, let's move fast" or when the question pops up unexpectedly in a technical screen. It hits the same three beats — present, past, future — in roughly 60 words.

## Practice protocol

Record yourself saying your version on your phone. Play it back. If you wince, the answer isn't ready. The goal is *fluent but not rehearsed-sounding* — you should be able to ad-lib the connecting tissue while keeping the structure rigid. Most people need 8-10 recorded takes before the answer feels natural. Do this once at the start of your search and you never have to do it again for that role.
