# 01 — Salary negotiation

> Negotiation • Position 1/2

## What

The end-game conversation after you've passed the interview loop. The company has decided to hire you and is now offering a package; you have *one* short window — usually 1-2 weeks — to negotiate before signing.

## Why it matters

A 10% bump on the initial offer compounds across base raises, bonuses tied to base, equity refreshes, and your next role's market data anchor. Over a 4-year tenure that's often $40-200K (or ₹15-60L) of forgone income for a 30-minute conversation. Recruiters and hiring managers *expect* you to negotiate; not doing so doesn't earn you brownie points, it just costs you money.

## The mindset

- **You're not adversaries — you're closing a deal.** The recruiter wants you to sign. Their job depends on closing offers; this is structurally cooperative.
- **They have rooms.** Almost every company has band flexibility, separate sign-on budget, equity refresh discretion, and a "competing offer" lever. The initial number is a starting point, not the ceiling.
- **Calm > clever.** The single most powerful move is silence after stating a number. Don't fill it.

## The structure: Anchor → Counter → Close

### Step 1 — Anchor

When the recruiter asks early on about salary expectations, **deflect or give a range** based on *their* market, not yours.

- **Don't disclose your current salary** (illegal to ask in many US jurisdictions; generally a bad idea anyway because it caps the offer).
- If pressed, give a researched range based on Levels.fyi / Glassdoor / Blind for that level + location. Make the range realistic for the upper-end target you actually want.

> Script: *"I'd rather understand the full scope first. Based on the market for this role and level, my expectation is in the $X to $Y range, with the actual number depending on equity and other components."*

### Step 2 — Counter

After the verbal offer arrives, **say thank you, ask for it in writing, ask for time**.

> Script: *"Thanks — this is exciting. Can you send the details in writing so I can review them properly? I'd like a few days to discuss with my family, and I'll come back with any questions early next week."*

Then research — Levels.fyi, your network, competing offers if you have them — and prepare your counter. The counter should target the **package**, not just one number:

> Script (text or short email): *"Thanks again for the offer. I'm genuinely excited about [team / mission]. Based on competing conversations and market data for this level, I was hoping we could land somewhere closer to **$X base + $Y sign-on + Z RSU/4y**, with a level review at 12 months. Is that workable?"*

Why this works: you've named specific numbers, given a credible reason (competing conversations + market data), and offered a face-saving fallback (level review). Recruiters can take this back to compensation.

### Step 3 — Close

Once they come back, decide what's enough. If they're 80% of the way to your target, that's often acceptable. Closing well builds the relationship for your first day.

> Script: *"That works for me — thank you for getting this to a great place. I'll sign the offer tomorrow. Looking forward to joining the team."*

## Specific scripts for tricky moments

### "What are your salary expectations?" (early)
> *"I'd want to understand the full scope first. Could you share the band for this level?"*

If they push: name a researched range at the upper end of your real target.

### "What is your current salary?"
> *"I'd prefer to focus on what makes sense for this role rather than anchor to my current package. My target for this kind of role is $X-$Y."*

### "We can do $X — can you accept today?"
> *"$X is meaningful, thank you. I want to make sure I'm thoughtful about a multi-year decision, so let me come back to you within 24-48 hours after thinking it through and discussing with my family. I'm not playing games — I'd just like the proper time."*

Never accept on the call. *Especially* if they pressure for an immediate answer — that pressure itself is a signal there's room.

### "We don't normally do sign-on bonuses."
> *"I understand. Could we make up the gap with extra equity, or a 6-month review for a level adjustment? My current package has [equity vesting / deferred bonus / RSU cliff] I'd be walking away from, so I want to bridge that."*

### "This is our best and final offer."
> *"I appreciate you saying that. Can I share the two specific things that would make it easier for me to accept — the base and the sign-on — and you can tell me what's possible? If neither is doable, I'll still consider what's here carefully."*

Frequently "best and final" is not. Recruiters are trained to say it. Stay polite, name the two specific items, see what happens.

### You have a competing offer
> *"I want to be transparent: I have an offer from Company X with $A base + $B sign-on + $C RSU. I'd prefer to join you for [team / mission / role reason]. Is there room to close the gap?"*

Only do this with a real offer. Bluffing gets caught and burns the relationship.

### You don't have a competing offer
You can still negotiate. Use market data (Levels.fyi, levels.fyi screenshots, your prep and the time you've invested).

> *"I've researched levels and packages carefully, and for this role and location at this level, the median seems closer to $X. Could we move toward that?"*

## What's usually negotiable

| Lever | Typical room |
| --- | --- |
| Base salary | 5-15% |
| Sign-on bonus | $10-50K (or ₹3-15L) — often a separate budget |
| Equity / RSU | 10-30% — recruiters have refresh-budget discretion |
| Start date | Almost always flexible — useful to delay vesting cliff |
| Target bonus % | Sometimes, especially at senior levels |
| Title / level | Rarely on the spot; sometimes via a 6-12 month review clause |
| Relocation / WFH allowance | Often discretionary |

## What's usually not negotiable

- 401(k) match, health insurance, PTO policy (these are HR-wide, not per-offer).
- Vesting schedule structure (4-year, 1-year cliff is standard for US tech; sometimes monthly vesting after cliff is open to negotiate).
- Equity strike price for new joiners (fixed by the most recent valuation).

## Anti-patterns

- **Disclosing current salary.** Caps the upside.
- **Accepting on the first call.** You lose your only leverage moment.
- **Anchoring low.** If you say "I'm hoping for $150K" and they were ready for $200K, you just lost $50K.
- **Negotiating only base.** Sign-on + equity are often easier to move.
- **Being aggressive or threatening.** "I have three other offers and you need to match" doesn't work. "I'd love to join — can you help me close the gap?" does.
- **Going dark while deciding.** Communicate. "I'll have an answer by Friday" is fine. Silence isn't.

## Cheat-line

**Anchor late, counter specifically, close politely.** The single highest-leverage move you'll make in a 4-year window is the 30 minutes between the verbal offer and the signed letter.
