# 02 — Evaluating offers

> Negotiation • Position 2/2

## What

Once two or more offers are on the table, you need to compare them on a real apples-to-apples basis. Total comp at face value almost always lies — vesting cadences, dilution risk, level differences, and life fit all hide in plain sight.

## Why it matters

Two offers that *look* identical can differ by 30-40% in expected value once you account for vesting curves, equity risk, and level. And total comp isn't the only axis — the wrong manager or the wrong product can cost you years of growth.

## The 5-minute spreadsheet

Open a fresh spreadsheet. One column per offer. Rows:

| Component | Notes |
| --- | --- |
| Base salary | Year-1, year-2, year-3, year-4 — base raises differ across companies. |
| Sign-on bonus | Year-1 only typically; sometimes clawback if you leave early. |
| Target bonus % × base | Whether it's paid at 100% historically — ask. |
| RSU grant total | Divided across the actual vesting cadence (next row). |
| RSU vesting curve | 25/25/25/25? 33/33/34? 6/19/25/30/20? Each one is wildly different. |
| Equity refresh assumption | Will you get an annual refresh? At what %? |
| 401(k) match / NPS | Often 3-6% of base. |
| Sign-on clawback | If you leave at 12 months, do you pay it back? |
| Year-1 total | Sum row. |
| Year-2 total | Sum row. |
| Year-3 total | Sum row. |
| 4-year total | The number that matters. |
| **Risk-adjusted equity** | Multiply private/pre-IPO equity by your risk discount (see below). |

Then add a second block of non-comp comparisons:

| Factor | Offer A | Offer B |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Level / title | E5 vs SDE-2 — can affect next role's anchor |
| Manager — do you trust them? | From skip-level conversation |
| Team — interesting work? | Concrete projects in flight |
| Product trajectory | Growth vs maturity vs decline |
| Office vs WFH / hybrid | Including return-to-office risk |
| Commute / relocation | Time + money cost |
| Health insurance quality | Especially if dependents |
| PTO + parental policy | Real PTO, not "unlimited" theater |

## RSU vesting curves matter more than you think

Two RSU grants of $400K over 4 years can deliver dramatically different cash flows depending on the curve.

| Curve | Year 1 | Year 2 | Year 3 | Year 4 |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **25/25/25/25** (Google, Meta) | $100K | $100K | $100K | $100K |
| **33/33/34 over 3y** (Netflix-ish) | $133K | $133K | $134K | — |
| **6/19/25/30/20** (Amazon style — 5-year-ish with backloading) | $24K | $76K | $100K | $120K | $80K |
| **Front-loaded sign-on heavy** (some startups) | $50K + $30K sign-on | $90K | $130K | $130K |

Amazon's backloading is especially important to model: if you only stay 18-24 months, you'll get a fraction of what the headline number suggests, *and* a clawback on the sign-on.

## Private / pre-IPO equity — risk-adjust it

A startup grant of $200K/year of RSUs and an offer at a public company of the same number are **not** comparable. Apply a discount:

| Stage | Suggested discount | Why |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Public, liquid | 0-5% | RSUs vest into cash; modest tax/lock-up risk |
| Late-stage private (Series E+, near IPO) | 20-40% | Will probably IPO but timeline + price uncertain |
| Mid-stage (Series C-D) | 40-70% | Real upside but real chance of zero |
| Early stage (Seed-A-B) | 70-95% | Treat as a lottery ticket; salary alone should justify |
| Pre-product startup | ~99% | Take it for non-comp reasons |

Strike price, dilution, and exit scenarios for ISO/ESOP grants in private companies all add complexity. If equity is a major component, talk to a tax advisor / friend who's been through an IPO before signing.

## Level / title matters more than 5% comp

If Offer A is L4 at $300K total, and Offer B is L5 at $290K total, B is probably better. Why:

- Your next role's compensation anchors to your current level + title.
- Promotion velocity at the *new* company starts from your current level, not from $0.
- Being underleveled means you'll be passed over for project ownership, on-call rotations, mentoring opportunities — the stuff that matters for promo.

The exception: if A's manager and team are dramatically better, take A. Career growth > 5% comp in any given year.

## Non-comp factors that *do* compound

- **Manager.** A great manager unlocks two grade-jumps over a 4-year window. A bad manager can flatline a strong engineer.
- **Product trajectory.** A team on the way up gives you more impact stories to put on your next resume.
- **Engineering culture.** Code review quality, test discipline, post-mortem hygiene. These compound on your skills.
- **Adjacency to learning.** Are there senior engineers around you who'll mentor — explicitly or by osmosis?

## Anti-patterns

- **Comparing year-1 totals.** Year-1 lies because of sign-on. Compare 4-year totals.
- **Treating private equity at face value.** $1M of pre-IPO RSUs is not $1M of cash.
- **Ignoring the manager.** Your day-to-day depends more on this human than the comp.
- **Optimizing only for comp.** Two years of misery at a 20% premium isn't worth it.
- **Refusing the lower offer without asking it to match.** Always ask. Recruiters often have refresh budget.

## When the offers are close

If you genuinely can't decide on comp grounds, the tiebreaker should be:

1. **Manager + team** (single biggest factor in your day-to-day).
2. **Product / company trajectory** (affects your next move).
3. **Learning opportunity** (do you grow technically?).
4. **Lifestyle fit** (commute, WFH, hours culture).

Comp is the entry ticket. Everything else is what you live with.

## Cheat-line

**Compare 4-year totals, risk-adjust private equity, and never let a 5% comp gap outvote a great manager.**
